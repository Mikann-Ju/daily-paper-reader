Abstract 

Large language models (LLMs) are increas-ingly deployed as autonomous agents capa-ble of executing multi-step action trajecto-ries toward a given objective. While exist-ing safety research has focused on detecting unethical behavior from complete trajectories, this paradigm is fundamentally retrospective: it identifies harm only after it has already oc-curred. In this work, we study a critical yet overlooked safety task, which we term Pre-dictive Monitoring : given only a partial ac-tion trajectory, can a model infer whether it will culminate in an unethical action before 

the overt action is executed? To support this task, we present PreAct-Bench , a benchmark of 1,000 paired ethical and unethical action trajectories spanning five domains. We evalu-ate a range of LLMs, safety guardrail models, and latent probing methods across varying frac-tions of the action trajectory using our Prefix Foresight F1 metric. Results show that while humans achieve promising performance, pre-dictive monitoring remains challenging even for strong models, highlighting the need for future-oriented risk reasoning in LLM safety. 1

Re-cent work has shown that LLMs can exhibit strate-gically misaligned or deceptive behaviors, such as pursuing harmful side objectives while evading detection (Scheurer et al., 2024; Hubinger et al., 2024). Figure 1 illustrates this gap: in the admissions example, actions such as publish-ing criteria, revising rubric weights, and discussing applicant–faculty fit each appear procedurally legit-imate, yet collectively suggest covert preparation to pre-select candidates tied to specific faculty. To study LLMs’ capability for anticipatory safety mon-itoring, we propose the Predictive Monitoring task: given a partial trajectory free of overt unethical actions, a model infers whether the trajectory will culminate in an unethical behavior. Based on the Predictive Monitoring task, we construct PreAct-Bench , comprising of 1,000 step-by-step action trajectories spanning five real-world domains: academia, law&contract, cybersecurity, politics, and daily life. Each trajectory is designed such that early steps appear ethical in isolation, yet serve as stealthy preparation for an overtly unethi-cal action. We validate these characteristics using our stepwise stealthiness and intention coherence 

metrics. During inference, the model is provided only with steps preceding the overt unethical action, rendering the task inherently predictive. Using PreAct-Bench , we evaluate a range of ap-proaches, including off-the-shelf LLMs, guardrail models, and linear probing. Beyond safety classi-fication, we also evaluate LLMs’ ability to judge the likelihood that a trajectory culminates in an un-ethical action. Finally, to understand the unique challenges posed by PreAct-Bench , we analyze the predictive monitoring difficulties, evaluate LLMs’ performance in the wild, and examine the capabil-ity gap between LLMs and human annotators. In summary, our contributions are threefold: • We introduce predictive monitoring , a novel task requiring models to infer latent misalignment risk from seemingly benign trajectories. • We introduce PreAct-Bench , a dataset of 1,000 trajectories across five domains. • Our analysis reveals that LLMs struggle with early-stage risk detection, perform poorly in the wild, diverge from human likelihood judgments, and degrade in reasoning mode.

Safety Monitoring Methods. LLM monitors are developed to detect the underlying misalignment via observable actions, tool calls, model outputs, reasoning traces, or internal activations. General guardrails such as Llama Guard (Inan et al., 2023), WildGuard (Han et al., 2024), ShieldGemma (Zeng et al., 2024), and Qwen3Guard (Zhao et al., 2025) classifies risks in prompts, responses, or input-output pairs. Agent-focused methods such as AGrail (Luo et al., 2025) and Pro2Guard (Wang et al., 2025) monitor proposed actions or partial execution traces. Other approaches use privileged signals: chain-of-thought monitoring can detect re-ward hacking (Baker et al., 2025), and latent probes can identify deceptive behavior from internal rep-resentations (Goldowsky-Dill et al., 2025). These methods are complementary: PreAct-Bench pro-vides a prefix-only benchmark for testing whether such monitors can detect latent risk before any unsafe action or state becomes visible. 3 Construction of PreAct-Bench 

3.1 Task Definition: Predictive Monitoring 

We formally define Predictive Monitoring , the task underlying PreAct-Bench . Given a task , situation ,and a trajectory for accomplishing the task, pre-dictive monitoring treats the trajectory as partially observable by truncating it before the first overt unethical action. The goal is to predict whether the prefix actions will culminate in an unethical action. To distinguish predictive monitoring from post-hoc detection, we define the overt exposure step k†

as the step where the first explicit unethical action occurs. Let the complete trajectory be represented as X = ( x1, . . . , x T ), where xt denotes the t-th step and k† ≤ T is the index of the overt unethical step. In post-hoc detection, a model has access to the complete trajectory X, including the overt unethical actions. In predictive monitoring, the model can only access the partial trajectory X≤k

for some k < k † for the future outcome prediction. Given the inherently probabilistic nature of fu-ture prediction, we define two label taxonomies for the task: binary , indicating whether the whole trajectory is E THICAL or U NETHICAL , and prob-abilistic , a 0–5 Likert scale rating denoting the likelihood of the partial trajectory leading to mali-cious acts, where 0 indicates certainly ethical and 5 indicates certainly unethical .

3.2 PreAct-Bench Creation Pipeline 

Each PreAct-Bench entry comprises a background 

(e.g. situation, task, and hidden objective ) and an action trajectory . We present the overall con-struction pipeline shown in Figure 2, which en-sures the quality of both the generated task and the corresponding trajectory. Specifically, we first construct a task from a situation and a hidden objec-tive, then generate paired trajectories for the same task: ethical and unethical . This paired design reduces confounds by holding the surface task fixed while varying the underlying intent. To ensure data quality, we conduct both automatic quality assur-ance and human annotation. The remainder of this section describes these steps. 

Situation, Task, and Hidden Objective. We focus on five real-world domains: academia, law&contract, cybersecurity, politics, and daily life. For each domain, we generate three domain-specific components shown in Figure 2 (left): situ-ations defining the agent’s role and context, tasks setting neutral and expert objectives, and hidden objectives driving unethical behavior (e.g., biased PhD admissions). For the law&contract, we prompt LLMs to summarize the background and task from ContractNLI (Koreeda and Manning, 2021), a document-level natural language inference dataset on contracts; for all other domains, situation, and task descriptions are generated by direct prompt (prompts in Appendix A8). We demonstrate the diversity of generated tasks in Appendix B. 

Paired Trajectory Generation. Based on the sit-uation and task, we generate paired ethical and unethical (driven by the hidden objective) trajecto-ries for the same surface task, as shown in Figure 2 (middle). In unethical trajectory generation, we balance two competing requirements : individual steps must appear ethical in isolation, while collec-tively advancing the hidden objective. We explic-itly define these criteria in the trajectory generation prompt (Figure A9) and propose two automated metrics to verify the generated data (§3.3). We use GPT5-mini as the data generator. 33.3 Data Refinement and Validation Stepwise Rewriting. After the initial generation, we observed that unethical steps often retain lin-guistic cues of the underlying hidden objective, such as “presented as” , “frame as” , causing them to read as distinctly more malicious than a neutral baseline. To address this, we identify and replace these cues with neutral equivalents, using LLMs to revise the text for fluency. To preserve the origi-nal step structure, we apply an edit-distance–based filter that rejects modifications altering more than 25% of the words. This process effectively miti-gates spurious correlations in the trajectories. See Appendix C for further details. 

Quality Assurance. To further enhance dataset quality, especially the two competing requirements ,we measure the Stepwise Stealthiness and Intention Coherence of generated trajectories. Stealthiness reflects how ethical each step appears in isolation 2.Intention coherence measures how closely each step aligns with the underlying malicious objec-tive. Both scores are determined using GPT-4o as a judge. Evaluation prompts are in Appendix F. We calculate both scores across trajectory steps, with shaded bands showing the cross-domain vari-ance. As shown in Figure 3, we observe that: (i) Step-level stealthiness of unethical trajectories re-mains relatively high with a slight decrease when in the later stage, on par with ethical counterparts, indicating that individual steps are not indicative of a malicious outcome. (ii) The intention coher-ence of unethical trajectories is up to 79%, which is approximately 2 × higher than their ethical coun-terparts, confirming that these seemingly benign steps are contextually related to the hidden objec-tive. These results validate that PreAct-Bench suc-cessfully maintains a latent ethical tension : indi-vidual steps appear indistinguishable from ethical behavior, yet are contextually anchored to a hid-den malicious objective, such that innocent actions collectively serve a harmful goal.

4 Experiments 

We first evaluate how well existing LLM monitors detect potentially misaligned outputs on PreAct-Bench (§4.2), comparing against existing misalign-ment benchmarks. We then assess models’ capa-bility in a realistic deployment setting where prob-abilistic reasoning and sensitivity to misaligned content are critical (§4.3). Finally, we analyze the key design choices and the pitfalls of reasoning mode in safety monitoring (§4.4).

4.2 Evaluate Results of LLM Monitors LLM Monitors on PreAct-Bench . Table 3 shows the overall evaluation result. Humans performed strongly, with an average PFF(100) of 0.907, con-firming the feasibility of the proposed task and the quality of the data entries in PreAct . For open-sourced LLMs, larger models of the same family generally perform better at predictive monitoring. Guardrail models’ poor performance on PreAct in-dicates that training on explicit adversarial trajec-tories fails to generalize to predictive monitoring. While supervised linear probing achieves adequate performance, it still falls short of prompting LLMs. This performance gap is particularly notable given that representations were extracted from a large open-source model (Llama-3.3-70B); by compari-son, Gemma3-27B, a newer, smaller open-source model, outperforms it with an average PFF(100) score of 0.839 versus 0.774. Most importantly, our results show that enabling reasoning consistently degrades model performance , a gap that generally widens with scale. This is evident from an aver-age degradation of 0.082 in Qwen3-0.6B, which escalates to a 0.208 drop in Qwen3-30B-A3B. This phenomenon is particularly concerning as enabling reasoning mode is becoming a de facto setting in most mainstream models, which, according to our results, makes the model more vulnerable to mali-cious usages. We conduct an in-depth analysis of the failure modes of reasoning models in §4.4.

The Base-Rate Fallacy. The base-rate fallacy 

arises when the rarity of the malicious event causes detectors with high nominal performance to pro-duce many false alarms among the benign pop-ulation (Kahneman and Tversky, 1973; Koehler, 1996). In LLM safeguarding, malicious user queries only account for roughly 8% of all queries in real-world deployments 8 (Lin et al., 2023; Zhao et al., 2024). Consider 100 user queries, 8 of which are malicious. A detector with 90% sensitivity and 90% specificity would correctly flag approxi-mately 7 malicious queries, but would also incor-rectly flag about 9 of the 92 benign queries. Thus, among all flagged queries, only 44% would actu-ally be malicious. Despite apparent strong sensi-tivity and specificity, the detector’s positive pre-dictions would therefore be more likely to be in-correct. To account for the base-rate fallacy, we evaluate both the reliability of positive and negative predictions. Specifically, we report Precision@ π

and False Negative Rate (FNR). Precision@ π cap-tures the probability that a flagged query is truly malicious given the deployment-relevant base rate (π = 8% ). FNR reflects the proportion of unethi-cal entries that are erroneously classified as ethical.

5 Conclusion 

We introduce the novel Predictive Monitoring task, which requires models to detect misalignment risk from partially observed action trajectories before overtly unethical action materializes. To support this task, we present PreAct-Bench , a benchmark 8of 1,000 paired ethical and unethical trajectories across five domains, designed so that individually defensible prefix steps collectively create precondi-tions for a covert unethical goal. Evaluating various baselines reveals that model performance degrades substantially at smaller window sizes, and safety guardrail models fail to generalize to predictive monitoring. Meanwhile, strong human annotator performance confirms that the task is tractable and that the dataset is of high quality. Together, these results highlight a fundamental gap in current LLM safety evaluation and open new research directions for anticipatory risk reasoning. 

Ethics Statement 

This work introduces PreAct-Bench , a benchmark designed to evaluate the ability of LLMs to detect misalignment risk from partial action trajectories. We discuss the ethical considerations relevant to its construction and use. 

Dual-use risk. PreAct-Bench contains paired ethical and unethical trajectories in which covert harmful intent is embedded within sequences of individually defensible steps. While this design is essential for evaluating predictive monitoring, the same trajectories could in principle inform the con-struction of deceptive agent behaviors. We note, however, that the situations in our benchmark are synthetically generated, domain-general, and ab-stract in nature, and do not provide operational in-structions for causing harm. We believe the safety benefit of enabling rigorous evaluation of predic-tive monitoring capabilities outweighs the marginal dual-use risk. 

Intended use. PreAct-Bench is intended for re-search on LLM safety evaluation, specifically the development of predictive monitoring methods. It should not be used to train models to produce de-ceptive or misaligned behaviors, nor to evaluate systems in high-stakes deployment settings with-out additional safeguards. 

Broader impact. We hope this benchmark con-tributes to the development of more proactive LLM safety mechanisms, reducing reliance on post-hoc detection and enabling earlier intervention before unethical actions materialize. 

Limitations 

Synthetic Trajectory Generation. All trajecto-ries in PreAct-Bench are synthetically generated us-ing GPT-5-mini, which introduces a potential circu-larity: the same model family is used for both data construction and evaluation labeling. Although we validate trajectory quality through human annota-tion and show reasonable human–GPT agreement (α = 0.624), synthetic trajectories may systemati-cally differ from the organic deceptive behaviors that would arise in real deployed agents, potentially overstating or understating the difficulty of the task in practice. 

Domain and Language Coverage. PreAct-Bench spans five domains (law and contract, pol-itics, cybersecurity, academia, and daily life), all in English. While the word-cloud analysis in Ap-pendix B demonstrates meaningful intra-domain diversity, the benchmark does not cover important high-stakes domains such as healthcare, military, or financial trading, nor does it address multilingual settings. Generalization of our findings to these settings remains an open question. 

Inherent Subjectivity. Inter-annotator agree-ment is moderate (average Krippendorff’s α ≈

0.47 for both binary and ordinal setups). This re-flects genuine ethical ambiguity in many trajecto-ries rather than annotation noise alone. The binary label taxonomy necessarily flattens this ambiguity, and the probabilistic 0–5 Likert scale only partially addresses it. 

Trajectory Length and Structure. Trajectories contain between 5 and 10 steps by construction. Real-world agentic tasks may involve substantially longer action sequences, and it is unclear how the difficulty of predictive monitoring scales with tra-jectory length or with the positional distance be-tween early preparatory steps and the eventual overt unethical action.

The regex rules fall into three categories. 1. Clause removal : entire clauses containing framing language are deleted. Patterns are capped at 5 words to avoid removing too much text. 2. C.3 Stage 3: Edit Distance Guard
